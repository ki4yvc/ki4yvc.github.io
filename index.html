WHAT MAKES AN AMERICAN

By Dylan Wilcox | ENGL 2140

IN AMERICA,

We take great pride in our identity often focusing on the superficial things, our politics, our football teams, where we go church. But at the heart is that where we are, given how much we divide ourselves amongst part lines? What do texts written in the past reveal about how we overcome these things and write our identity as Americans?

THE FOCUS

Though written in different time periods, Crevecoeur's What is an American? (1782), Richard Wright's The Man Who Was Almost a Man (1961) and Robert Jones' The Collapse of American Identity (2017) all give clues to how we as American's overcome our struggles and differences to write our own identify.

  • Crevecoeur - What is an American?

    "What then is the American, this new man? He is either an European, or the descendant of an European, hen ce that strange mixture of blood, which you will find in no other country... He becomes an American by being received in the broad lap of our great Alma Mater." (Crevecoeur 312)

  • Richard Wright - The man Who Was Almost a Man

    "Ahead the long rails were glinting in the moonlight, stretching away, away to somewhere, somewhere where he could be a man." (Wright #)

  • Robert Jones - The Collapse of American Identity

    "This end is not inevitable, but if we are to continue to make one out of many, leaders of both parties will have to step back from the reactivity of the present and take up the more arduous task of weaving a new national narrative in which all Americans can see themselves." (Jones)

THE REASONING

An analysis of the previous quotes.

BECOMING

Crevecoeur illustrates that an American is made by people from all backgrounds and of all religions. America is a melting pot of all religoins in his case, but in the future this will become all races, all genders. He may not mention the future conflicts that may be in store, but this is the root of all out identies, accepting everyone is the core of writing our own identity.

CHALLANGES

Wright shows us that Dave is challanged by many different facets, from his race to the gun. Though he has made a share of mistakes on his journey, somewhere in this country theres a place for him. When put into the big picture, wright could be suggestions how even though as a country we have made tough decsions and been through tough times somewhere down the line we find out way, much the same way Dave can go be a man.

OVERCOMING

Jones points out in his article that today we face struggle with our identiy as Americans. Everywhere we look issues such as LGBT, race and education are divided by party lines. As Jone's wrote in his aritcle, we've overcome divesiveness once before and we can do it again. Much the same way Dave was able to step away from his present to look down the rails, Jone's tells us to step back and weave a new identity.

PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

As Jones describes in detail in his article, Americans have long been diveded in very polar ways. In Wright's The Man who Was Almost A man the racial tensions between black and white are obvious. In all these storiesm, however; that true identify of an American is revieled, and that is as a people no matter what divesiveness we face we can pull through, we can come back to that moment the Crevecoeur describes, that moment where everyone can come together and be American. Sometimes, as in the case of the Civil war, we are looking down the tracks, and today our differnces are causing us to once again look down those tracks.

IN CONCLUSION.

Jones warns us of the dangers of the conflict we are struggling with and how its causing us to loose the American identity. Crevecoeur defines it as everyone coming into a melting pot, and sticking with it together while Wright tells us an example of such stuggle and starting over. We can choose to start down new tracks and write the American identity we want.
